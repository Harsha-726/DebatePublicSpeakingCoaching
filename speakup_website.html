Core Principles

What We Believe About
Young Debaters

Every element of SPEAK UP! is grounded in six principles — ensuring debate is inclusive, educational, and genuinely empowering for 4th graders.

🧠

Every Kid Has Good Ideas

We start from the belief that every child has something worth saying. Our job is to give them the tools to say it clearly — never to replace their ideas with ours.

👂

Listening Is Half of Debate

Great debaters are even better listeners. You can only argue against what you truly understand. Respect begins with attention.

🎯

Structure Creates Freedom

Rules don't limit expression — they liberate it. When students know the structure, their ideas flow freely within it, like music on a staff.

🌱

Progress Over Perfection

We celebrate growth, not winning. A student who overcame stage fright wins more than a polished speaker who wasn't challenged. We track personal bests.

🤝

Disagree Without Being Disagreeable

One of life's most valuable skills: opposing someone's idea while respecting the person. A strong rebuttal always acknowledges before it attacks.

✨

Fun Is the Engine

Games, skits, role-play, and laughter are baked into every lesson. If students don't want to come back next week, we haven't done our job.

Full Curriculum

7 Units · 30 Lessons

Each unit builds on the last, creating a deliberate scaffold from personal voice to full competitive debate. Every lesson includes activities, discussions, and hands-on practice.

01
Unit One
Weeks 1–2 · 4 Lessons
Finding Your Voice
Unit Goal

Students overcome stage fright, discover their natural speaking voice, and develop foundational habits: eye contact, volume, pace, and posture.

1.1
Hello, World!
Ice-breakers, classroom norms, and the "Why We Debate" discussion. Everyone shares one thing they believe strongly.
Ice-Breaker
1.2
The Four Pillars of Speaking
Introduce Volume, Pace, Eye Contact, and Posture via silly warm-ups: "Robot Voice", "Sloth Pace", "Statue Stare".
Mirror Practice
1.3
My 60-Second Story
Students prepare and deliver a 60-second speech about their favorite thing (no notes). Feedback is positive-only this session.
First Speech!
1.4
Stage Fright & The Butterfly Trick
Normalize nervousness. Teach 3 breathing techniques. Students rate anxiety before/after. Discuss how butterflies become fuel.
Reflection Journal
02
Unit Two
Weeks 3–4 · 4 Lessons
Building Arguments
Unit Goal

Students learn the P.E.E. framework (Point → Evidence → Explanation) and practice turning opinions into structured, persuasive arguments.

2.1
Opinion vs. Argument
What's the difference between "I like cats" and "Cats make better pets because..."? Students sort statements into Opinion vs. Argument cards.
Card Sort Game
2.2
The P.E.E. Framework
Introduce Point → Evidence → Explanation. Build arguments using the "Argument Burger" diagram (bun = point, patty = evidence, sauce = explanation).
Argument Burger!
2.3
My First P.E.E. Speech
Each student writes and delivers one 90-second P.E.E. argument on a topic of their choice. Peers give "glow & grow" structured feedback.
Peer Feedback
2.4
Claim vs. Counterclaim Preview
Every argument has an opposing argument. Students try to argue FOR and AGAINST the same position (e.g., "Should we have homework?").
Two-Sides Game
03
Unit Three
Weeks 5–6 · 4 Lessons
Evidence & Research
Unit Goal

Students understand the difference between facts and opinions, identify strong vs. weak evidence, and practice age-appropriate research for debate preparation.

3.1
Fact, Opinion, or Lie?
Fast-paced game: teacher reads 20 statements and students vote with paddles. Introduces verifiable vs. subjective claims.
Voting Paddles
3.2
What Makes Good Evidence?
Compare strong evidence (statistics, expert opinion) vs. weak evidence (rumors, "my mom said"). Students rank evidence cards strongest to weakest.
Evidence Ranking
3.3
Mini Research Lab
Students research a debate topic using teacher-curated articles. Find 2 pieces of evidence for your side and 1 against — knowing both sides matters.
Library Session
3.4
The Source Detective
Who said it? Why trust them? Explore source credibility through a "Is This Real?" mystery game with fake vs. real sources.
Mystery Game
04
Unit Four
Weeks 7–8 · 4 Lessons
The Art of Rebuttal
Unit Goal

Students master the RREA rebuttal structure (Repeat → Refute → Evidence → Alternative) and learn to respond in the moment with grace and accuracy.

4.1
What Is a Rebuttal?
Watch and analyze two short clips of respectful real-world disagreement. Identify what worked and what didn't. Introduce RREA.
Video Analysis
4.2
Repeat Back — Show You Listened
Practice paraphrasing your opponent's argument before countering. "What my opponent said was..." Drill until it becomes instinct.
Paraphrase Drills
4.3
Refute & Replace
Find the weak spot in an argument, attack the assumption, offer a better explanation. "That's wrong because... and instead..."
Attack the Gap
4.4
Rebuttal Rapid Fire
Speed round: teacher reads a claim, student has 30 seconds to deliver a full RREA rebuttal. No notes. Celebrate attempt over accuracy.
Rapid Fire!
05
Unit Five
Weeks 9–10 · 4 Lessons
Debate Formats & Rules
Unit Goal

Students learn Oxford-style and Team Policy Lite debate formats, understand speaker roles and time limits, and practice being fair judges.

5.1
Oxford-Style Debate Intro
Proposition vs. Opposition, one speaker each, audience votes before and after. Full mock debate on "Pizza is better than tacos."
Mock Debate
5.2
Team Debate Format
Introduce 2v2 team format: 1st Speaker (constructive), 2nd Speaker (rebuttal), summary. Everyone debates. Small teams rotate.
Team Roles
5.3
Being a Fair Judge
Students learn the 4 judging criteria: Argument, Evidence, Delivery, Rebuttal. Practice scoring a teacher-performed demo debate.
Judging Sim
5.4
Debate Ethics & Sportsmanship
No interrupting, no personal attacks, accept the judge's decision. Role-play "bad sportsmanship" scenarios and fix them as a class.
Ethics Role-Play
06
Unit Six
Weeks 11–13 · 6 Lessons
Practice Rounds
Unit Goal

Students apply all skills in three structured practice debates across progressively harder topics, with emphasis on feedback, reflection, and personal improvement goals.

6.1
Practice Debate #1 — Easy Topic
"School should start later in the day." Full Oxford format with notes allowed. Structured peer feedback forms used afterward.
Practice Round
6.2
Debrief & Growth Goals
Each student watches their own 60-second clip. Identifies 1 strength and sets 1 specific improvement goal for next debate.
Self-Review
6.3
Practice Debate #2 — Medium Topic
"Animals should not be kept in zoos." Assigned sides — students argue what they're given, not what they believe. No notes for 1st Speakers.
Practice Round
6.4
The Rebuttal Workshop
Targeted coaching on students' weakest area (based on rounds 1 & 2). Small groups. Instructor models strong rebuttal on their own argument.
Targeted Coaching
6.5
Practice Debate #3 — Hard Topic
"Social media does more harm than good." Full 2v2 format. 20 min research, then debate. Closest to tournament conditions.
Practice Round
6.6
Tournament Preparation
Students receive their tournament topic 1 week early. Guided prep: research plan, argument construction, anticipate opposition, write cue cards.
Prep Session
07
Unit Seven
Weeks 14–15 · 4 Lessons
Mini Tournament
Unit Goal

The culminating experience: a structured 3-round mini-tournament with a panel of judges, certificates for all, and a full celebration of growth and achievement.

7.1
Tournament Day 1 — Rounds 1 & 2
Morning: All pairs debate Round 1. Scores tallied. Feedback. Afternoon: Round 2 with reshuffled pairings for balanced competition.
🏆 Tournament
7.2
Tournament Day 2 — Round 3 + Finals
Round 3 for all. Top 2 pairs advance to a Final Round. Full audience watches the final. All students serve as audience judges.
🏆 Finals!
7.3
Awards & Celebration
Certificates for ALL students. Special awards: Best Rebuttal, Most Improved, Best Evidence, Most Enthusiastic. Peer appreciation circle.
🎉 Celebration
7.4
Looking Forward — Closing Reflection
Students write a letter to their future selves about what they learned about speaking, listening, and thinking. Discuss debate in everyday life.
Final Reflection

Assessment

How We Evaluate Students

Assessment is growth-centered. Students are never graded against each other — only against their own previous performance and the rubric below.

Category 4 Excellent 3 Developing 2 Beginning 1 Needs Support
Argument Quality Clear P.E.E. structure, strong relevant evidence, logical explanation P.E.E. mostly present, evidence somewhat relevant Has a point and some evidence, explanation unclear Mostly opinions, little or no evidence
Delivery Strong volume, excellent eye contact, confident pace throughout Good volume most of the time, some eye contact Quiet or rushed, limited eye contact, reads most of speech Very difficult to hear, reads entirely from notes
Rebuttal Accurately restates opponent's point, clear counter + evidence Generally addresses opponent's argument, counter mostly clear Attempts rebuttal but misrepresents opponent's point Does not engage with opponent's argument
Listening & Respect Attentive throughout, takes notes, respectful at all times Mostly attentive, no disruptive behavior Some inattention or minor interruptions Frequently distracted or shows poor sportsmanship

Culminating Event

The Mini Tournament Format

A safe, structured, and celebratory competition where every student wins something — and every student grows.

R1

Round 1 — Open Pairs

All students debate. Random pairings. Topic revealed 20 minutes before. Oxford style, 3 minutes per speaker.

R2

Round 2 — Balanced Draw

Reshuffled to match similar R1 scores. Same topic, opposite sides — testing adaptability.

R3

Round 3 — New Topic

Fresh topic, 30-minute prep. Closest to real competition. Top 2 scores advance to finals.

🏆

Finals — Full Audience

Two best pairs face off in front of parents, teachers, and classmates. Audience votes for fun — judges decide officially.

🎖️

Awards Ceremony

Every student receives a certificate. Special recognition for Best Rebuttal, Best Evidence, Most Improved, Most Sportsmanlike.

👨‍⚖️

Judge Panel

3-person panel per debate: 1 instructor, 1 parent volunteer, 1 older student mentor. Verbal feedback given after each round.

Instructor Guidance

Best Practices for Facilitators

Tips drawn from experienced debate coaches who have worked with elementary-age students. The classroom dynamic at this age requires a fundamentally different approach.

✅ Always Do

  • ✅Start every session with a 5-minute warm-up game (tongue twisters, vocal exercises, "hot take" opinions)
  • ✅Celebrate attempts, not just success — cheer when students try hard things
  • ✅Give feedback as "glow + grow" — one compliment before every suggestion
  • ✅Let students choose their own argument topics whenever possible — buy-in is everything
  • ✅Assign students to the OPPOSITE side of their real opinion at least once — perspective-taking is the whole point
  • ✅Use movement: standing, moving to sides, physical voting — 9-year-olds cannot sit still for 45 minutes
  • ✅Record speeches when possible — self-review is the most powerful feedback tool at this age
  • ✅Frame debate as a life skill, not just competition — reference examples from school, home, and everyday news

🚫 Never Do

  • 🚫Mock or laugh at a student's argument — even unintentionally, this ends their participation
  • 🚫Let the same 3 confident students dominate — cold-call quieter students with gentle, supportive prompts
  • 🚫Introduce topics that are divisive at the family level (immigration, religion, abortion) — save those for high school
  • 🚫Skip the debrief — rushing past reflection loses 40% of each lesson's learning value
  • 🚫Focus only on winning — a student who delivered a beautiful but losing rebuttal deserves the most praise in the room
  • 🚫Allow personal attacks in rebuttals — shut these down immediately and use them as teachable moments for the class
  • 🚫Go straight to formal debate before students feel psychologically safe — safety and rapport always come first

Topic Bank

Recommended Debate Topics
for 4th Grade

All topics are age-appropriate, genuinely two-sided, and engaging for 9–10 year olds. Organized by difficulty for progressive use throughout the year.

🟢 Beginner — Fun & Concrete
  • 📚 Students should have less homework
  • 🐶 Dogs make better pets than cats
  • 🎮 Video games should be allowed in school
  • 🍕 Pizza is the best food
  • ⏰ School should start later in the day
  • 🌳 Kids should spend more time outdoors
🟡 Intermediate — Real World Issues
  • 🦁 Zoos should be banned
  • 📱 Kids under 12 should not have phones
  • 🏫 All schools should have dress codes
  • ♻️ Everyone should be required to recycle
  • 🥩 People should eat less meat
  • 🏆 Winning is more important than participating
🔴 Advanced — Complex & Nuanced
  • 💻 Social media does more harm than good
  • 🤖 AI in the classroom helps students learn
  • ⚽ Schools should cut sports to save money
  • 🌍 Rich countries should help poor countries more
  • 🏙️ Cities are better places to live than small towns
  • 🧪 Animals should not be used in science experiments
